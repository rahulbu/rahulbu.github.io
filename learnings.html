Notes

Short takes from building Gen AI on top of real systems.

The model is the easy part

Everyone obsesses over which LLM to use. But whether an AI feature actually ships comes down to the unglamorous engineering around it — clean data, retrieval quality, latency, observability, and the judgment to know when not to reach for a model at all. The teams that win with Gen AI aren't chasing the newest model; they're the ones who can wrap a reliable system around it.

RAG is a search problem

Most "our AI gives wrong answers" bugs aren't model bugs — they're retrieval bugs. If you're building RAG, the model is maybe 20% of the quality; the rest is putting the right context in front of it: chunking that respects meaning, hybrid keyword + vector search, and re-ranking before anything reaches the model. Years of Elasticsearch turned out to be more useful here than anything AI-specific.

When not to use an LLM

The most senior move in AI engineering is often saying "we don't need an LLM for this." Plenty of "AI features" are really a regex, a SQL query, or a small rules function — faster, cheaper, deterministic, and debuggable at 2am. LLMs are great for genuinely fuzzy, language-shaped problems; they're an expensive choice for problems that have a correct answer you can compute. Reliability is a feature.
